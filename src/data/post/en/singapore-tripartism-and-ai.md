> Lever 3 (Talent) on this site gives you a clean but partial picture: workers in the AI era equal people whom the government pays to retrain. This piece supplies the other half — how tripartism absorbs the shock that AI delivers to the labour market. But a fact has to be put on the table first.

## 1. Read this first: NTUC is not an independent union

Singapore's NTUC Secretary-General has long been a current or former Cabinet minister. Lim Swee Say held the role from 2007–2015, Chan Chun Sing 2015–2018, Ng Chee Meng from 2018. Tripartism — Government + NTUC + SNEF — is therefore not an adversarial bargaining mechanism. It is the architecture through which the PAP state internalises the labour organisation and semi-internalises the employer side (SNEF is more institutionally independent but deeply cooperative).

This is the key fact for reading anything below.

**The cost of symbiosis** — workers have no genuinely adversarial leverage. The hard, union-led pushback on algorithmic management or automation-driven layoffs that you see in parts of Europe does not happen here. The state sets the agenda; the unions execute. When Pritam Singh proposed legislating retrenchment benefits, Tan See Leng's parliamentary reply was that "legislation is not a panacea" — that is the standard response of this mechanism to more activist labour demands.

**The dividend of symbiosis** — the unions can mobilise state-scale resources for sectoral retraining. Company Training Committees reach thousands of firms; SkillsFuture interlocks with NTUC LearningHub; the Platform Workers Act granted statutory collective representation to over 70,000 platform riders. That scale of worker-side intervention is not buildable in a free-market polity.

How to use the frame: tripartism is not a channel for workers to "have a voice." It is the apparatus by which AI's labour-market shock is absorbed in negotiated form. Its strength lies in the state paying and employers participating; its blind spot is that workers have no exit option and do not set the agenda.

## 2. The three fronts Lever 3 cannot see

Lever 3 tucks MOM, WSG, and SkillsFuture into the functional slot of "the government pays for retraining." That is real, but it is only one face of the AI × labour question. Tripartism works on three other fronts —

### Front 1: Hiring fairness (AI recruitment bias)

Singapore passed the **Workplace Fairness Act**, upgrading the soft Tripartite Guidelines on Fair Employment Practices (TGFEP) into statutory protection. It covers age, gender, race, religion, family status, disability, mental health, and nationality.

The statute does not name AI directly — but Josephine Teo at the ATX Summit was explicit: AI-driven hiring bias already falls under the Workplace Fairness Act, and a dedicated AI law is not required. This is a typical expression of Singapore's AI regulatory philosophy: **anchor risk on protected classes (the victim dimension), not on technology classification (the perpetrator dimension)**.

Enforcement runs through TAFEP (the Tripartite Alliance for Fair Employment Practices), which investigates complaints and refers disputes. But Patrick Tay's 2024 parliamentary question exposed an awkward fact: at that point, no AI hiring-tool bias complaints had been recorded.

Three readings of the zero-complaints number:

1. AI hiring tools really do have no problems.
2. Workers do not know they were filtered out by an algorithm.
3. The TADM/TAFEP channel is not recognised by complainants as the place to bring an "AI" issue.

A blind spot in Singapore's AI governance may be hiding here. Even so, tripartism completed the move from soft guideline to hard statute — a pre-emptive institutional upgrade for the AI era.

### Front 2: Algorithmic management (Platform Workers Act)

The **Platform Workers Act** is one of the world's rare laws to confront algorithmic management directly. For the 70,000-plus riders and drivers on Grab, foodpanda, and Deliveroo it provides:

- Mandatory CPF contributions
- Mandatory work-injury insurance
- **Statutory collective representation** — through NTUC-affiliated Platform Work Associations

The third item is the unusual one. It pulls the previously labour-law-exempt category of "algorithmically managed worker" back into the tripartite frame, meaning platform companies must negotiate working conditions with NTUC-linked associations.

But can these associations actually negotiate over the AI scheduling algorithm itself? Not yet. The 2025 agenda is dominated by CPF rates, work-injury coverage, payment protection on platform exit, and road safety. **Algorithmic transparency, appeal mechanisms, and contextual fairness (peak-hour, high-risk-route algorithm behaviour) — the core AI × labour issues — have not yet entered the formal table.**

NTUC MP Yeo Wan Ling already put "algorithmic management" on the table in the Committee of Supply 2026 debate (COS-MOM): "AI can augment workers or replace them, depending on how work and roles are redesigned." She demanded that Industry Transformation Maps (ITMs) explicitly chart AI-based business-process redesign, workforce-transition timelines, and "credible pathways" into new roles when current jobs are obsoleted.

A signal that tripartism is starting to put "algorithm vs. human" on the explicit agenda. But not yet at the bargaining layer.

### Front 3: AI dividend redistribution (PWM and industry transformation)

Something Lever 3 does not surface: **Singapore's tripartism has a unique dividend-redistribution device** — the Progressive Wage Model (PWM).

PWM locks sectoral wage growth to skill ladders — when unit productivity rises, worker wages must rise in step. Budget 2026 expanded the Progressive Wage Credit Scheme (PWCS) co-payment subsidy: firms get fiscal support, but the condition is wage increases for local employees.

The deeper meaning: when AI lifts unit productivity in a covered sector, PWM forces a meaningful share of that gain back into wages. This is not the free-market story of "AI raises productivity but worker wages stagnate." It is a redistribution device unique to tripartism.

Where is the boundary? PWM today covers cleaning, security, landscaping, retail, food services, lift maintenance, and administrative work — **all low-skill, labour-intensive sectors**. The AI shock to the PMET middle class (Professionals, Managers, Executives, Technicians) has no PMET-version PWM yet. That is an open policy gap.

## 3. Three watchpoints for 2026–2028

The decisive observations for whether tripartism can bear the weight of AI acceleration:

**1. Whether the Senior Employment Tripartite Workgroup's output truly absorbs the AI mid-career squeeze**

DPM announced the workgroup at Budget 2026, with recommendations expected within the year. AI automation hits mid-to-late-career PMETs hardest — this workgroup's output is the key test of tripartism's depth in the AI era. If the recommendations stay at "extend the senior employment tax credit" levels of marginal adjustment, the mechanism's capacity to handle structural change will be limited.

**2. Whether the Platform Work Associations can collectively bargain on algorithmic issues**

Today's bargaining sticks to non-algorithmic items — CPF, work-injury, payment guarantees, road safety. If 2026–2028 produces something like "NTUC Platform Work Association demands Grab disclose key scheduling-algorithm parameters" or analogous collective action, the mechanism is reaching into the algorithm itself. Three more years of silence would mean the Platform Workers Act conferred formal representation without substantive algorithmic bargaining power.

**3. Whether Workplace Fairness Act enforcement actually catches algorithmic discrimination**

The law has passed and is rolling out. The first AI-algorithm-discrimination cases — if they appear — will tell us whether "anchor risk on protected classes" can hold up in the AI era. Three more years without an AI-linked case throws the question back to the "zero complaints" puzzle: blind spot or genuine non-issue, still under observation.

## 4. Levers drive; tripartism absorbs

Back to the opening picture: Lever 3 sees the government's supply side — talent development, retraining, transitions, all packaged into a state-funded pipeline. Real, but incomplete.

The full social contract of Singapore's AI model = **the state-to-enterprise drive apparatus (the 6 levers) + the tripartite shock-absorption apparatus (this piece)**. Stack the two layers and you get the middle road that distinguishes Singapore from US market laissez-faire and PRC administrative compulsion.

Symbiosis is both ceiling and engine:

- **Ceiling**: workers have no exit option, the agenda is set by the state, and AI issues will not produce genuinely militant labour challenges.
- **Engine**: negotiation can mobilise state resources, employer cooperation, union execution — redistributing AI's dividend and shock inside the institution.

Which face dominates will determine whether Singapore's next five years on AI × labour is a "negotiated AI welfare state" or a "negotiated AI inequality reproduction." Both endings remain inside the logic of negotiated authoritarianism. The fork lies in whether tripartism can reach into the algorithm itself, and whether it can extend protection to new shock cohorts like PMETs.

Lever 3 cannot see this side. This piece is about exactly that side.
